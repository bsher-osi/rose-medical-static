Syncope in Children: When Fainting Needs a Neurologist

By Dr. Tamara Zach MD — May 05, 2026

Understanding Syncope in Children: More Than Just "Passing Out"

It's one of the most frightening things a parent can witness — your child suddenly collapses, loses consciousness, and goes limp. Whether it happens at a school assembly, during soccer practice, or standing in line at a Phoenix summer event, a fainting episode leaves families shaken and full of questions. Is this serious? Could it happen again? Does my child need to see a specialist?

At Rose Medical Pavilion, Dr. Tamara Zach MD works with families across the Phoenix area to answer exactly those questions. Syncope — the medical term for a temporary loss of consciousness caused by reduced blood flow to the brain — is one of the more common reasons children are referred to a pediatric neurologist. While many cases are benign, some require careful evaluation to rule out underlying neurological or cardiac causes.

What Causes Syncope in Children?

Not all fainting episodes are the same, and the underlying cause matters enormously when it comes to treatment and safety planning. The most common types seen in pediatric patients include:

Vasovagal (Neurocardiogenic) Syncope

This is by far the most frequent type in children and teenagers. It occurs when the nervous system overreacts to a trigger — such as standing too long, emotional stress, pain, or the sight of blood — causing a sudden drop in heart rate and blood pressure. The child may feel lightheaded, nauseous, or notice their vision going gray before losing consciousness briefly. While vasovagal syncope is generally not dangerous, it can be disruptive and occasionally leads to injury from falls.

Orthostatic Hypotension

When a child stands up quickly and feels faint, it may be due to a temporary drop in blood pressure. This type of syncope is particularly common in rapidly growing adolescents and can be worsened by dehydration — a real concern here in Phoenix, where summer temperatures routinely exceed 110°F. Inadequate fluid intake during outdoor activities significantly increases the risk of orthostatic episodes in Arizona children.

Cardiac Syncope

Less common but more serious, cardiac syncope occurs when an abnormal heart rhythm or structural heart problem temporarily reduces blood flow to the brain. Warning signs include fainting during exercise (rather than after), fainting without any warning sensation, or a family history of sudden cardiac death. These cases require urgent evaluation and close collaboration between a pediatric neurologist and a cardiologist.

Syncope vs. Seizure: A Critical Distinction

One of the most important roles a pediatric neurologist plays is distinguishing syncope from a pediatric seizure. During a syncope episode, some children experience brief jerking movements or muscle twitches as the brain is briefly deprived of oxygen. To witnesses, this can look exactly like a seizure — and understandably, many families arrive at the emergency room with a seizure diagnosis when the actual event was syncopal. Dr. Zach is experienced in taking a detailed clinical history to differentiate between the two, which directly impacts the evaluation and management plan.

How Is Syncope Evaluated in a Pediatric Neurology Setting?

When a child is referred to Dr. Tamara Zach MD at Rose Medical Pavilion for a loss of consciousness event, the evaluation is thorough and tailored to the individual child's history and presentation. A detailed account of the episode — what the child was doing beforehand, any warning symptoms, how long they were unconscious, and how they felt afterward — provides crucial diagnostic clues.

Diagnostic testing may include:

  • Electroencephalogram (EEG): When there is any concern that the event may have involved seizure activity, a pediatric EEG can help identify abnormal electrical patterns in the brain. This non-invasive test is an important tool for distinguishing epileptic events from syncope.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG): To evaluate for cardiac rhythm abnormalities that could be contributing to fainting episodes.
  • Tilt table testing: In cases where vasovagal syncope is suspected but diagnosis is uncertain, this specialized test can reproduce and confirm the mechanism.
  • Blood work: To check for anemia, blood sugar irregularities, or electrolyte imbalances, all of which can contribute to fainting.

When Should You See a Neurologist for Child Fainting in Arizona?

Not every fainting episode requires a specialist visit immediately, but certain situations warrant prompt neurological evaluation. Seek care from a pediatric neurologist if your child:

  1. Fainted during physical activity or exercise
  2. Lost consciousness without any warning signs or prodromal symptoms
  3. Had jerking movements, eye deviation, or prolonged confusion after the episode
  4. Has had multiple fainting episodes over weeks or months
  5. Has a personal or family history of heart problems
  6. Sustained a head injury during the fall — in which case pediatric concussion care may also be warranted
  7. Is experiencing associated symptoms such as severe headaches, vision changes, or neurological complaints

Managing Syncope in Arizona's Climate

Living in the Phoenix metro area presents unique challenges for children prone to syncope. Extreme heat accelerates dehydration and causes blood vessels to dilate, both of which lower blood pressure and increase fainting risk. Dr. Zach routinely counsels Arizona families on practical strategies: aggressive hydration with electrolyte-containing fluids before and during outdoor activities, avoiding prolonged standing in heat, wearing light and breathable clothing, and recognizing early warning symptoms so children can sit or lie down before a full episode occurs.

For school-aged children with recurrent syncope, coordinating a management plan with the school nurse — and ensuring teachers and coaches understand the condition — is an important part of keeping children safe and academically active.
