Sleep Disorders in Children: More Than Just Bedtime Struggles

Home › Blog › Sleep Disorders in Children: More Than Just Bedtime Struggles

By Dr. Tamara Zach MD — May 04, 2026

When Sleep Problems Are More Than a Phase

Every parent has weathered a rough night — or a rough week — of their child refusing to sleep, waking repeatedly, or dragging through the next day. But when sleep struggles become a persistent pattern, they deserve more than reassurance that your child will "grow out of it." For many Arizona families, what looks like a simple bedtime battle is actually a neurological issue that responds well to proper evaluation and treatment.

At Rose Medical Pavilion in Phoenix, Dr. Tamara Zach MD works with children and families across the Valley to identify the root causes of pediatric sleep disorders — and to build solutions that actually work. Here's what every Phoenix-area parent should know about sleep disorders in children.

Why Sleep Matters More for Children Than You Might Think

Sleep isn't passive downtime for a child's brain — it's when critical work happens. During deep sleep, the brain consolidates memories, regulates mood, clears metabolic waste, and supports healthy neurological development. Chronic sleep deprivation in children has been linked to attention problems, behavioral difficulties, learning delays, and even worsened seizure control in children with epilepsy.

The American Academy of Sleep Medicine recommends the following nightly sleep totals for children:

  • Infants (4–12 months): 12–16 hours (including naps)
  • Toddlers (1–2 years): 11–14 hours (including naps)
  • Preschoolers (3–5 years): 10–13 hours (including naps)
  • School-age children (6–12 years): 9–12 hours
  • Teenagers (13–18 years): 8–10 hours

If your child consistently falls short of these targets — or meets them but still seems unrefreshed — a neurological evaluation may be an important next step.

Common Sleep Disorders Seen in Children

Insomnia

Child insomnia is one of the most frequently misunderstood sleep disorders. It isn't simply a child who doesn't want to go to bed — it's a persistent difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or waking far too early, despite having an appropriate opportunity and environment for sleep. In Phoenix's extreme summer heat, many families notice sleep patterns worsening between June and September, when nighttime temperatures remain above 90°F and air conditioning cycling can disrupt light sleepers. True insomnia in children often has neurological underpinnings and may coexist with anxiety disorders, ADHD, or autism spectrum disorder.

Obstructive Sleep Apnea

Snoring might seem harmless, but pediatric obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) — where the airway partially or fully collapses during sleep — is a serious condition affecting roughly 1–5% of children. Children with OSA often present with loud snoring, restless sleep, nighttime sweating, and daytime behavioral problems that can closely mimic ADHD. A neurological evaluation helps distinguish OSA-related attention difficulties from primary attention disorders and ensures children receive the right treatment.

Parasomnias: Night Terrors, Sleepwalking, and Confusional Arousals

Parasomnias are disruptive events that occur during sleep transitions. Night terrors, sleepwalking, and confusional arousals are most common in children between ages 2 and 12 and are generally benign — but they can be frightening for families and occasionally dangerous. Importantly, some parasomnia-like episodes may actually represent nocturnal seizure activity. Dr. Tamara Zach MD carefully evaluates these events to distinguish parasomnias from epileptic episodes, sometimes using a pediatric EEG to capture brain activity during sleep and rule out underlying seizure disorders.

Restless Legs Syndrome and Periodic Limb Movement Disorder

Children with restless legs syndrome (RLS) often describe an uncomfortable urge to move their legs at night — though younger children may not have the words to explain it and instead simply seem unable to settle. Periodic limb movement disorder (PLMD) involves repetitive limb movements during sleep that fragment rest without the child fully waking. Both conditions are underdiagnosed in pediatric populations and have a neurological basis that warrants specialist attention.

Narcolepsy

Narcolepsy is a chronic neurological disorder affecting the brain's ability to regulate sleep-wake cycles. In children, it often presents differently than in adults — excessive daytime sleepiness may look like laziness or depression, and cataplexy (sudden muscle weakness triggered by emotion) is frequently subtle. Narcolepsy requires specialist diagnosis and management, and early identification can significantly improve a child's quality of life and school performance.

The Connection Between Sleep and Other Neurological Conditions

Sleep disorders rarely exist in isolation. Children with pediatric epilepsy have significantly higher rates of sleep disruption, and poor sleep can in turn lower seizure thresholds — creating a difficult cycle that requires coordinated neurological care. Similarly, children who experience frequent pediatric headaches or migraines often report that poor sleep is a primary trigger, while headache disorders themselves can disrupt sleep architecture.

A neurologist specializing in pediatric sleep disorders understands these overlapping systems and can evaluate your child's sleep health within the broader context of their neurological wellbeing.

What to Expect at a Pediatric Sleep Evaluation

When you bring your child to Rose Medical Pavilion for a sleep concerns evaluation, Dr. Zach will begin with a thorough clinical history — reviewing sleep patterns, medical history, family history, medications, and daytime functioning. Depending on the presenting concern, evaluation may include:

  1. A detailed sleep diary review
  2. Validated sleep questionnaires appropriate for your child's age
  3. A pediatric EEG if nocturnal seizure activity is suspected
  4. Coordination with sleep medicine specialists for polysomnography (overnight sleep study) when indicated

For families enrolled in AHCCCS (Arizona's Medicaid program), many pediatric neurology evaluations — including those related to sleep disorders — are covered benefits. Families of young children with developmental concerns may also be connected with AzEIP (Arizona Early Intervention Program) resources when appropriate.
